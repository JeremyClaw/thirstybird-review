Tab 1

What ThirstyBird is, why it matters, and what the website should communicate

Product summary

ThirstyBird is a mobile platform that allows users to pre-purchase discounted drinks at licensed venues such as bars and restaurants, with each offer tied to a specific time window set by the venue.

The platform helps venues drive traffic during off-peak hours by offering controlled, time-based promotions, while giving consumers an easy way to discover deals, save money, and skip payment friction when ordering.

Long-term, ThirstyBird can become a scalable marketplace connecting consumers, venues, and eventually alcohol brands — turning fragmented promotions into a measurable and repeatable growth channel for hospitality.

What the current presentation under-communicates

  • That this is more than a deal directory — it is a structured demand-generation platform for venues.
  • That pre-purchase removes friction and creates a better customer experience at the bar.
  • That timed promotions are the core mechanic and key differentiator.
  • That there is a future media / brand / growth platform opportunity underneath the consumer app.

Tab 2

Full app UI / UX audit from the user perspective

Based on the mobile flow reviewed across home, search, categories, filters, deal detail, cart, wallet, profile, and empty states.

7 / 10

Overall assessment

ThirstyBird is structurally solid and easy enough to understand, but it is under-designed relative to the strength of the business idea. The core opportunity is less about fixing broken usability and more about improving hierarchy, trust, brand identity, and emotional pull.

Home / Browse

7.5 / 10
  • Working: simple browsing, clear search, usable categories, easy scanning.
  • Issue: weak top-of-screen value prop; the app opens with listings, not a powerful reason to care.
  • Recommendation: add modules like Live now, Ending soon, Best nearby, and strengthen the message around prepaid convenience and timed offers.

Categories + Search

7 / 10
  • Working: obvious search bar, familiar category entry points.
  • Issue: category treatment feels generic and visually underpowered.
  • Recommendation: improve iconography, active states, and add more user-driven sort/filter options like On now, Ends soon, Best value.

Deal cards

6.5 / 10
  • Working: readable cards, discount visible, venue/time/price all present.
  • Issue: hierarchy is too soft; cards explain but do not sell.
  • Recommendation: push discount, time urgency, original vs discounted price, and venue trust much harder.

Deal detail

7 / 10
  • Working: strong image, visible CTA, valid times and venue info included.
  • Issue: detail page is functional but not persuasive enough; terms feel a bit admin-heavy.
  • Recommendation: add reassurance like instantly added to wallet after payment and improve redemption clarity.

Cart

7.5 / 10
  • Working: easy to understand, checkout CTA clear, Peach Payments trust cue helps.
  • Issue: visually plain for such an important commitment moment.
  • Recommendation: add stronger purchase reassurance, total savings emphasis, and clearer post-payment expectations.

Wallet + Profile

7 / 10
  • Working: right IA and tab logic, profile is clean and understandable.
  • Issue: both surfaces feel too generic; wallet should feel like a flagship utility, profile like a value hub.
  • Recommendation: improve active/redeemed voucher states, strengthen support/help clarity, and make savings feel more rewarding.
Priority actions

What the developer should tackle first

High priority

1. Rework home-screen hierarchy

Make the app’s core promise obvious in the first few seconds. Less generic listing feed, more guided discovery.

High priority

2. Redesign offer cards

Stronger discount emphasis, urgency, pricing contrast, and trust signals.

High priority

3. Upgrade the visual system

Move from generic light marketplace UI to a premium nightlife / hospitality identity.

Medium priority

4. Improve detail-page reassurance

Clarify redemption, wallet delivery, and what happens after payment.

Medium priority

5. Improve filter logic

Add user-intent filters like On now, Ends soon, Best value, Closest, Most popular.

Medium priority

6. Treat wallet as a product feature

Active / used / expired states should be clearer, more premium, and easier to trust at a glance.

Tab 3

What the product should feel like instead

The goal is not to make ThirstyBird flashy for the sake of it. The goal is to make the product feel as valuable as the business idea actually is.

Target design direction

  • Premium nightlife utility, not generic marketplace
  • Richer contrast and stronger hierarchy
  • Warmer hospitality palette with more appetite and energy
  • Offer cards that feel merchandised, not merely listed
  • Wallet and redemption states that feel trusted and useful
  • Microcopy that explains redemption clearly without admin heaviness

App redesign principles

How I’d translate this into the actual app

1. Home should guide behaviour

Prioritise live deals, urgency, and local relevance over a flat feed of offers.

2. Cards should sell

Stronger pricing contrast, better deal naming, clearer timing, and more persuasive scan paths.

3. Darker premium mode

A richer hospitality palette can make the brand feel more memorable and premium.

4. Wallet should feel bulletproof

Voucher state design should optimise confidence, quick scanning, and in-venue usability.

5. Profile should create loyalty

Make savings, referrals, and support feel like product value — not just settings.

6. Copy should remove doubt

Explain payment, redemption, and validity in calm, direct, user-first language.

Next build step

If this direction is approved, the next move is redesigned app screens and a production-ready marketing homepage.
